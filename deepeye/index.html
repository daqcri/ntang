Why?

A picture is worth a thousand words. A good visualization is worth a terabyte of data. Nowadays, the ability to create good visualizations has shifted from a nice-to-have skill to a must-have skill for all data analysts to help managers make business decisions, where data is the primary force behind for its high volume and overwhelming velocity. Despite the overwhelming choices of interactive data visualizaiton tools for experts, non-experts have poor choices for effective visualization recommendation systems such that everyone can easily create great visualizations.

What are the fundamental problems?

Visualization Recognition

Given one visualization, such as a bar chart or a scatter plot, is it good from understanding human perception?

Visualization Ranking

Given two visualizations, can we quantify them and say that which one is better?

Visualization Searching

It might be very hard, if not impossible, to guess what a user really wants. Can they do simple keyword like search?

How?

Our basic idea is "visualizatio by examples" â€“ there are plenty of generic priors to showcase great visualizations, which can be used to learn human perception, e.g., a bar chart with more than 50 bars is clearly bad.

Given thousands of good examples and some human labeled ranking orders, DeepEye trains a binary classifier (a random forest, or a SVM) for visualization recognition, and uses a supervised learning-to-rank model for visualization ranking.
